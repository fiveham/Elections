NC Partisan Primaries, Mar 3 2020

This interactive election map shows the districts and candidates for the March 3, 2020 partisan primaries in North Carolina. Candidates unopposed for their party's nomination are included in this map even though the offices for which they are running may not actually appear on their party's primary ballot.

How to Use

Choose a party to see the map through the lens of a specific party's primary. This removes candidates not of that party then removes contests without enough candidates of that party then finally dampens the color of districts with no remaining contests. This will also remove nonpartisan contests, even if they would appear on a party's primary ballot.

Change from one district set to another to see the districts of that type and get access to the contest/candidate info available on that layer.

Use the geolocator button to jump/zoom/snap to your location (as reported by your browser).

Why to Use

  • See which districts you live in
  • Learn the shape and extent of the districts you live in
  • Find out whether there are any weird district lines near you
  • Check who's running for which offices
  • Discover what offices are even up for election

Nonpartisan Contests

Some offices up for election do not pair candidates' names with party affiliation on the ballot. For these, this map lists candidates by name only.

Districts Within Counties and Municipalities

County commission and school board districts and districts within municipalities are not included in this map.

In some counties, commissioners are all elected at large, with no districts.

In some counties, commissioners are elected at large but nominated by district; so, districts don't matter to voters.

In some counties, commissioners are nominated and elected by district.

In Bladen county, commissioners are nominated and elected by district, but some commission seats are elected by the votes of multiple commissioner districts combined.

In some counties, some commission seats are elected at large while others are elected by district.

In some counties, school boards are elected at large. In others, school boards are elected by district.

Among the counties that elect school boards by district, some reuse their county commissioner district lines for the school board, too, but some use different district lines for commission and school board, and there may be some counties where school board members are elected by district while county commissioners are elected purely at-large with no districts to speak of.

Some counties' school board district maps may have holes in them where a municipal school board exists, voiding the county board's jurisdiction.

Accounting for low-level districts is a huge mess nearly impossible to do fairly; so, finding out which low-level districts apply to a given voter is left up to the reader to determine by examining their sample ballot in their voter registration lookup information on the NCSBE website once those are available.

Voter ID?

Not for the March 3rd primary, no.

The suit NAACP v. Cooper which led to the injunction blocking voter ID requirements for the primary, is ongoing and could wind up reinstating the requirements by the November 3rd general election.

Referenda, Bonds, Ballot Issues

Municipalities, counties, school districts, etc. may have yes/no questions on the ballot. These will probably not be included in this map simply because it is difficult to discover them all in a way that's fair and consistent across the state.

Election Incidents, Voter Intimidation

In the event of voter intimidation or other inappropriate stuff, there are several ways to report it as an election incident. Here are some:

  • ACLU Election Protection Hotline: 866-OUR-VOTE (866-687-8683)
  • Voting Rights Institute

Office and Candidate Sequence

Candidates for an office are listed with their party affiliation, sorted alphabetically. This typically differs from the order on the ballot. The candidate's party is listed before their name so alphabetization clusters candidates by party.

Statewide contests are sorted alphabetically within three unlabeled subsections: state executive offices, state judicial offices, then federal offices.

Incumbents?

The map does not indicate whether a candidate currently occupies the office they're running for.

Colors

The colors mean nothing, for the most part.

Orange and green were chosen to avoid partisan implications. Using a different brightness level for each color is meant to ensure districts can still be distinguished in case of color vision deficiency.

Lighter colors are used for shapes with no corresponding election on March 3rd, but the shades of green and orange are used purely to help distinguish adjacent shapes.
